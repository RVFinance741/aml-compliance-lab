A working reference for anti-money laundering practice. Covers core terminology, the ten typology patterns used in this simulator, and the primary regulatory frameworks governing suspicious activity reporting. Use this alongside the investigation queue to build contextual understanding alongside case-by-case judgment.

Core Terminology
Key definitions used across financial intelligence and transaction monitoring practice.
AML
Anti-Money Laundering
Click to expand
The body of laws, regulations, and internal controls designed to prevent criminals from disguising illegally obtained funds as legitimate income. AML programs typically comprise customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting obligations.
STR / SAR
Suspicious Transaction Report / Suspicious Activity Report
Click to expand
A mandatory regulatory filing submitted by financial institutions when activity raises reasonable grounds to suspect money laundering, terrorist financing, or related criminal activity. Filing thresholds and timelines vary by jurisdiction. In Canada, FINTRAC governs STR obligations; in the US, FinCEN governs SAR requirements.
KYC / CDD
Know Your Customer / Customer Due Diligence
Click to expand
The process of verifying a customer's identity, understanding their business activities, and establishing expected transaction behavior. CDD forms the baseline of a risk-based AML framework. Without an accurate customer profile, meaningful anomaly detection is not possible.
EDD
Enhanced Due Diligence
Click to expand
Additional scrutiny applied to higher-risk customers, relationships, or transactions. Typically triggered by PEP status, high-risk jurisdictions, adverse media, or unresolved transactional anomalies. Requires deeper inquiry into source of funds and source of wealth than standard CDD.
PEP
Politically Exposed Person
Click to expand
An individual who holds or has held a prominent public function, or who is closely associated with such a person. PEPs carry elevated inherent risk of bribery and corruption exposure and require enhanced due diligence and ongoing monitoring regardless of transaction volume or product type.
FATF
Financial Action Task Force
Click to expand
The intergovernmental standard-setter for AML and counter-terrorist financing globally. Publishes the 40 Recommendations, maintains jurisdiction-level risk classifications (grey list / black list), and conducts mutual evaluations of member countries. Jurisdiction-level risk ratings directly affect transaction risk scoring for cross-border wires.
Typology
Money Laundering Pattern
Click to expand
A documented method or behavioral pattern used to launder money or finance terrorism. Transaction monitoring systems are built around known typologies. Analysts are expected to recognize when observed activity resembles an established pattern and document that recognition in their disposition rationale.
Source of Funds / Wealth
SOF / SOW
Click to expand
Source of funds refers to the origin of specific transaction proceeds. Source of wealth refers to how a customer accumulated their overall assets. Both are central to EDD reviews and high-risk customer assessments — the analyst must establish whether the economic rationale is plausible given the declared profile.
Structuring
Also known as Smurfing
Click to expand
The deliberate act of breaking large cash amounts into smaller transactions to avoid mandatory reporting thresholds. Structuring is a stand-alone criminal offense in most jurisdictions, independent of whether the underlying funds are illicit. The pattern of intent — not the individual transaction — is the key indicator.
Placement, Layering, Integration
The Three Stages of Money Laundering
Click to expand
Placement: introducing illicit cash into the financial system. Layering: separating proceeds from their source through complex or cross-border transactions. Integration: re-introducing laundered funds into the legitimate economy in a form that appears clean. Transaction monitoring primarily addresses layering and placement indicators.
False Positive
System-Generated Alert with Legitimate Explanation
Click to expand
An alert that, upon analyst review, has a plausible and documented legitimate explanation. The majority of alerts in most institutions are false positives. Documenting the closure rationale clearly and specifically is essential — an undocumented closure on a genuine red flag is a regulatory exposure.
Beneficial Ownership
UBO — Ultimate Beneficial Owner
Click to expand
The natural person(s) who ultimately own or control a legal entity, or on whose behalf a transaction is conducted. Identifying beneficial ownership is a core CDD obligation, particularly for corporate, trust, and nominee structures where legal and beneficial ownership diverge.
Alert Typologies
The ten patterns used in this simulator — with investigative context for each.
Structuring
Typically High Risk — Reportable in most jurisdictions
Click to expand
Repeated transactions deliberately kept below a reporting threshold across consecutive dates or accounts. The intent to circumvent reporting is the offense — proximity to the threshold across multiple dates is the key signal. Question: is the pattern too regular to be coincidental?
Key indicators: Near-threshold amounts on consecutive days, cash-dominant activity, no apparent business rationale for the specific denominations.
High-Risk Jurisdiction Transfer
Medium–High Risk — Context and velocity dependent
Click to expand
Wires to or from jurisdictions flagged by FATF, the institution's risk appetite, or domestic regulators. Risk escalates with frequency, value, and when the destination is inconsistent with the customer's declared business or personal profile. Jurisdiction alone is not determinative — the combination of factors matters.
Key indicators: Destination inconsistent with business type, no documented commercial relationship, multiple wires to FATF grey or black list jurisdictions.
Money Mule Pattern
High Risk — Often reportable
Click to expand
Funds enter and exit the account rapidly with minimal retention. The account functions as a conduit rather than a genuine operating account. Frequently associated with fraud networks, trafficking, and organized crime. The account holder may or may not be aware of the role their account is playing.
Key indicators: Near-immediate outgoing transfers after incoming, multiple unrelated senders, negligible average balance, pattern inconsistent with declared occupation.
Dormant Account Reactivation
Medium Risk — Proportional to dormancy length and activity magnitude
Click to expand
A sudden resumption of high-value activity after a prolonged period of inactivity. The dormancy creates a gap in the behavioral baseline. Reactivation accompanied by cross-border transfers or large values is a recognized red flag in most AML typology libraries. The investigation should establish whether there is a documented trigger.
Key indicators: Dormancy period vs. activity magnitude, no documented trigger for reactivation, new counterparties with no established relationship.
Crypto Layering
High Risk — Emerging and evolving typology
Click to expand
The use of virtual asset service providers (VASPs) and crypto exchanges to move funds rapidly between crypto and fiat, often across multiple jurisdictions. The speed and pseudonymous nature of crypto facilitate the layering stage of the laundering cycle. FATF's updated guidance on VASPs has expanded reporting obligations in this area.
Key indicators: Rapid crypto-to-fiat conversion, no documented investment rationale, offshore transfer destinations, multiple exchange accounts.
Unusual Business Activity
Medium Risk — Profile and industry dependent
Click to expand
Transaction volumes, values, or counterparty types that deviate significantly from what would be expected given the business's declared size, industry, or stated purpose. The investigation should determine whether activity can be explained by legitimate business growth, a one-time event, or whether the profile itself requires updating.
Key indicators: Revenue inconsistent with business type, new counterparties in unrelated sectors, cross-border volumes disproportionate to declared business.
Round Number Wires
Lower Risk — Requires corroborating pattern evidence
Click to expand
Repeated wire transfers in clean, round denominations. Legitimate commercial transactions rarely produce perfectly uniform amounts consistently. Individually this carries limited weight, but when combined with other indicators — offshore destinations, no invoice documentation, regular frequency — it suggests scripted transaction behavior.
Key indicators: Identical amounts across multiple dates, no invoice or commercial agreement on file, regularity inconsistent with natural business activity.
Third-Party Funnel
High Risk — Often reportable
Click to expand
An account receiving funds from multiple unrelated third parties and forwarding them to other unrelated recipients — a classic funnel account structure. Frequently associated with fraud proceeds, underground banking, and informal value transfer networks. The account holder's relationship to the sending parties is the critical investigative question.
Key indicators: Multiple unrelated incoming parties, immediate onward transfers, no apparent account holder relationship to senders or recipients.
Sanctions Name Similarity
High Risk — Must be formally resolved before any action
Click to expand
A name-screening system alert where the customer's name matches or closely resembles an entry on a sanctions or watchlist. The analyst must make a documented false positive or true hit determination. A true hit requires immediate escalation and potential account action. An undocumented closure on a potential true hit is a serious regulatory exposure.
Key indicators: Review DOB, nationality, address, and any secondary identifiers. All resolution decisions must be documented with evidence, not assumed.
Transaction Spike
Medium–High Risk — Baseline and magnitude dependent
Click to expand
A sudden, significant deviation from the customer's established transaction baseline. The monitoring system flagged because the behavioral change cannot be explained by the existing profile data. The analyst must determine whether the spike has a legitimate cause — a property sale, business contract, inheritance — or whether it represents unexplained enrichment.
Key indicators: Magnitude of deviation, whether income profile supports the activity, whether the account has established counterparty relationships that explain the movement.
Regulatory Frameworks
Primary bodies and frameworks governing AML compliance obligations in North America and globally.
FATF
Financial Action Task Force
Click to expand
The global standard-setter for AML and counter-terrorist financing. The FATF 40 Recommendations establish the baseline obligations that member countries are expected to implement through domestic legislation. Jurisdictions that fail evaluations may be placed on the grey list (enhanced monitoring) or black list (call for countermeasures). These classifications directly affect transaction risk ratings in institutional AML models — a wire to a grey-listed jurisdiction carries elevated inherent risk regardless of amount.
FINTRAC
Financial Transactions and Reports Analysis Centre of Canada
Click to expand
Canada's financial intelligence unit and primary AML supervisor for reporting entities. Administers the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA). Reporting entities in Canada must file Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) and Large Cash Transaction Reports (LCTRs) for cash transactions of $10,000 CAD or more. FINTRAC's compliance expectations place significant weight on the quality and specificity of STR narrative documentation, not only the filing decision.
FinCEN
Financial Crimes Enforcement Network — U.S. Treasury
Click to expand
The US Treasury bureau responsible for AML policy and oversight under the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA). Requires filing of Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) and Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) above $10,000 USD. FinCEN issues typology guidance, administers beneficial ownership regulations, and has expanded virtual asset reporting obligations significantly since 2021. The Corporate Transparency Act (2024) has further extended beneficial ownership identification requirements across entity types.
AML Compliance Lab
v3.1 — The Modern Ledger Edition

AML Compliance Lab is an interactive transaction monitoring simulator designed for individuals building knowledge in financial crime compliance. It replicates the core analytical decisions an AML analyst encounters daily: reviewing system-generated alerts, assessing customer risk profiles, documenting investigation rationale, and determining whether observed activity warrants closure, escalation for enhanced due diligence, or a suspicious transaction report filing.

Cases are generated dynamically via a randomized scenario engine covering ten distinct alert typologies, realistic customer profiles including PEP classifications and name-screening flags, and variable jurisdictional risk across fifteen countries. No two sessions produce the same case queue. Performance is tracked per alert type, enabling targeted skill development over time.

The Reference Library provides contextual grounding for each pattern encountered — covering core terminology, typology-specific investigative guidance, and the primary regulatory frameworks (FATF, FINTRAC, FinCEN) that govern suspicious activity reporting obligations.
